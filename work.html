Selected Work

These are selected examples of strategy-to-deployment work across healthcare, enterprise AI, and operational analytics. The focus is on the problem, delivery challenge, my role, and the measurable or practical outcome.

Roche / GOSH partnership: multi-workstream applied AI leadership

Problem: Move clinically relevant AI opportunities from concept into delivery.

Challenge: Regulated context, interoperability constraints, and diverse stakeholders across clinical, product, and technical teams.

My role: Led cross-functional workstreams across prioritisation, technical direction, and delivery execution.

Outcome: Created practical delivery pathways across multiple AI opportunities while maintaining stakeholder alignment and momentum.

Why it mattered: It created a practical path for multiple clinically relevant AI opportunities to move forward in a regulated environment.

Public reference: Roche and Great Ormond Street Hospital.

Genomics document intelligence: PDF to FHIR to clinician dashboard

Problem: Genomics signal was locked in unstructured PDF reports, slowing clinical use.

Challenge: Heterogeneous language, mapping ambiguity, and interoperability requirements.

My role: Built the NLP pathway from extraction through FHIR mapping and clinician-facing dashboard delivery.

Outcome: Achieved an 80% reduction in manual review time and faster route from report intake to usable decision support.

Why it mattered: It turned information locked in unstructured reports into a workflow that could support clinician decision-making faster and more reliably.

Public reference: FHIR standard (HL7 FHIR).

CKD progression modelling

Problem: Support earlier risk awareness for CKD progression in paediatric care.

Challenge: Longitudinal data complexity and need for interpretability in potential clinical deployment.

My role: Co-led modelling and clinical translation with deployment pathway planning.

Outcome: XGBoost performed best with F1 0.72 and ROC AUC 0.80, with interpretability integrated into the modelling approach.

Why it mattered: It connected predictive modelling with interpretability and a plausible deployment pathway in a clinical context.

Public reference: publication list at Google Scholar profile.

Enterprise compliance RAG / LLM assistant

Problem: Internal teams needed reliable compliance and policy answers within daily workflows.

Challenge: Balancing usability with trust, traceability, and governance controls.

My role: Led product and delivery direction for the retrieval-augmented assistant and operating controls.

Outcome: Delivered enterprise capability for approximately 1,500 users with governance and traceability requirements built into usage.

Why it mattered: It showed that enterprise AI usefulness depends on trust, traceability, governance, and operating controls, not only model capability.

Public reference: practical RAG patterns (LangChain RAG overview).

Cancer Wait Times automation and MS prescribing analytics app

Problem: Operational and prescribing insights were slowed by manual reconciliation cycles.

Challenge: Fragmented datasets, naming inconsistencies, and repeat manual intervention.

My role: Led analytics automation and Streamlit app delivery using joins, fuzzy matching, and rule-based reconciliation.

Outcome: Improved repeatability and operational usefulness by reducing manual reconciliation and making fragmented data easier to work with.

Why it mattered: It improved repeatability and operational usefulness by reducing manual reconciliation and making fragmented data easier to work with.

Public reference: Streamlit and NHS waiting times context (NHS England RTT statistics).

More examples can be added over time across healthcare AI, enterprise AI, and analytics delivery.
